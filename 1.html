Introduction to CPU Scheduling

CPU Scheduling is a crucial function of an operating system that determines the order in which processes execute. It optimizes CPU utilization, improves system performance, and ensures fairness among processes.

Functionalities of CPU Scheduling

  • Maximizes CPU Utilization
  • Reduces Waiting Time
  • Ensures Fair Process Execution
  • Minimizes Response Time
  • Balances Load Among Processes

CPU Scheduling Algorithms

1. First Come First Serve (FCFS)

Processes are executed in the order they arrive. Simple but can lead to long waiting times for larger processes.

2. Shortest Job First (SJF)

Selects the process with the shortest execution time, reducing overall waiting time but may cause starvation for longer processes.

3. Longest Job First (LJF)

Opposite of SJF, where longer processes are prioritized, leading to shorter processes waiting longer.

4. Shortest Remaining Time First (SRTF)

Preemptive version of SJF where the shortest remaining time process is executed next.

5. Longest Remaining Time First (LRTF)

Preemptive version of LJF where the longest remaining time process is executed next.

6. Round Robin (RR)

Assigns a fixed time slice to each process and cycles through them, ensuring fairness but may have high context-switching overhead.

7. Priority Scheduling (Non-Preemptive)

Processes are executed based on priority. Higher priority processes execute first.

8. Priority Scheduling (Preemptive)

Similar to non-preemptive but allows interruption by higher priority processes.
