Longest Remaining Time First (LRTF) Scheduling

Introduction to LRTF Scheduling

Longest Remaining Time First (LRTF) is the preemptive version of LJF. It continuously selects the process with the longest remaining burst time.

LRTF is rarely used in general computing due to high average waiting times, but may be useful in specific scenarios where longer jobs should be prioritized.

Characteristics of LRTF

  • Preemptive: The current process can be interrupted if a new process with longer remaining time arrives.
  • High Average Wait Time: Shorter processes experience significant delays.
  • Starvation: Short processes may be delayed indefinitely if longer processes keep arriving.
  • Context Switching Overhead: High overhead due to frequent preemption.

How LRTF Works

LRTF continuously monitors the remaining time of all processes in the ready queue. Whenever a new process arrives, it compares its burst time with the remaining time of the current executing process. If the new process has a longer burst time, the CPU switches to it.

Example:

Consider three processes arriving at different times:

  • P1: Arrival: 0ms, Burst Time: 5ms
  • P2: Arrival: 1ms, Burst Time: 3ms
  • P3: Arrival: 2ms, Burst Time: 8ms

Execution order: P1 (0-2ms) → P3 (2-10ms) → P1 (10-13ms) → P2 (13-16ms)
