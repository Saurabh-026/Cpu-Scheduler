Round Robin (RR) Scheduling

Introduction to Round Robin Scheduling

Round Robin (RR) is a preemptive scheduling algorithm that allocates a fixed time slice (quantum) to each process in a circular queue. It's designed for time-sharing systems.

RR provides fair CPU distribution and responsive system behavior, making it one of the most widely used scheduling algorithms in modern operating systems.

Characteristics of Round Robin

  • Preemptive: Processes are forcibly switched after their time quantum expires.
  • Fair Allocation: Equal CPU time for all processes regardless of burst time.
  • No Starvation: Every process gets a turn to execute.
  • Time Quantum Selection: Performance heavily depends on the time quantum selection.

How Round Robin Works

Round Robin maintains a circular queue of processes. Each process is allocated a fixed time slice. If a process doesn't complete within its time quantum, it's placed at the end of the queue and the CPU switches to the next process.

Example:

Consider three processes with a time quantum of 2ms:

  • P1: Arrival: 0ms, Burst Time: 5ms
  • P2: Arrival: 1ms, Burst Time: 3ms
  • P3: Arrival: 2ms, Burst Time: 8ms

Execution order: P1 (0-2ms) → P2 (2-4ms) → P3 (4-6ms) → P1 (6-8ms) → P2 (8-9ms) → P3 (9-11ms) → P1 (11-12ms) → P3 (12-14ms) → P3 (14-16ms)
